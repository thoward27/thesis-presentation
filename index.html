Introduction

Abuse is a Problem

Abuse is a Problem

Regarding individuals with I/DD

  • 9/10 abused at least once
  • 50% experience 10+ instances
  • Often abused by known persons
  • Abuse is severely under reported
  • Valenti & Hein, sexual 1995
  • Venkatasubramanian, 2021
  • Known persons: care takers, family, friends
  • Under Reported: For example, Valenti & Hein found that only 3% of sexual abuse cases involving individuals with I/DD are ever reported
  • Faced with such a problem, what can we do?

Education as a Solution

  • Abuse prevention training is effective
  • Shown to increase confidence
  • Awareness and Action training
  • Logistics of attending in-person training
  • Frequency is important to continued understanding
  • Abuse prevention training has been shown to be effective in Krishna's work
  • Current solution: Awareness and Action in-person training events
  • In person trainings can be hard logistically to attend
  • Persons with I/DD may need help with transportation

Digitizing the A&A training

Digitizing the A&A training

  • Autonomous learning
  • Refreshing knowledge vs teaching
  • Emotionally Difficult Material
  • Intrinsic Motivation
Digitizing the training requires we circumnavigate a few crucial issues.
  • Can we design something someone can use autonomously?
  • If individuals need help and the abuser is their caretaker, they won't receive it.
  • What does it mean to refresh compared to teaching?
  • Teaching means the user may have never seen the material, whilst refreshing assumes they've been exposed already
  • How can we get individuals to return to the application enough to properly maintain their knowledge?

  • How are grounding activities built into the application received by the audience?

Collaborative Design

Collaborative design involves incorporating the target population in the design process for the application. We applied this technique in this work as an initial investigation to gauge effectiveness of 5 distinct medium-fidelity prototypes.

Gathering Requirements

Gathering Requirements

  • Generating Personas
  • Writing scenarios
  • Producing use-cases
  • Creating requirements
  • Developing prototypes
The first step to generating the required prototypes was to gather a proper set of requirements.
  • We generated 3 distinct personas for this work
  • Each persona represents a potential real user, though they are fictitious, emphasis was places on lifelike details
  • Each persona was then used to generate scenarios, informal narrative stories
  • These stories were then used to identify clear use-cases for the application
  • From these use cases, formal requirements were created that laid the foundation for the prototypes

Feedback: Emotional Reinforcement

"[the application should] never take anything away from somebody." - C5
  • Generally negative
  • Taking away happiness
  • Avoid negative reinforcement
  • Overall, this prototype faired quite poorly.
  • Participants expressed concern over the application taking away the happiness of the pet
  • They stressed that rewards should increase monotonically

Feedback: Grounding Activities

"it can be a little hard for people [to go through the training]" - C1
  • Very positive
  • All participants valued this element
  • Avoid mandatory fun
  • Add a time limit
  • This prototype faired extremely well in the session
  • All participants agreed this element was crucial
  • Designers suggested that we should avoid mandatory fun
  • also, add a time limit so that users don't get lost in the activity

Feedback: Interactive Elements

"Can you see the smoke coming out of my ears yet?" - C5
  • Generally positive
  • Confusing interface
  • Overly complicated
  • Move to discrete clips
  • Designers generally liked this concept
  • Although they found it confusing and overly complicated in its current incarnation
  • Instead of continuously playing the video, they suggested using discrete clips and asking after each

Feedback: Quizzes

"[it] should either, you know, talk about physical abuse or show the video" - C4
  • Very positive
  • Would benefit users
  • Not enough on its own
  • Make it optional
  • Overall this prototype was found to be effective
  • Designers thought it would benefit users
  • However, it was not enough on it's own
  • Designers stressed that you need to show before you ask questions
  • They also suggested making it optional as it was pointed out that if a user knows a quiz is coming they may feel anxious

Methodology

Methodology

  • Remote Synchronous Trials
  • Six participants with I/DD
  • Zoom Video Call
  • Shared virtual emulator
  • Remote control ceded where possible
  • Interview Questions + Verbal Feedback
  • We conducted these evaluations using remote synchronous trials
  • With the help of Mass Advocates Standing Strong we recruited six individuals with I/DD to participate
  • Evaluations were completed via zoom video calls
  • These sessions were bring-your-own-device, users logged in on whatever device they felt comfortable with
  • After logging in, we would share a virtual emulator running the application with them
  • and try to cede control, which worked if they were on a desktop computer
  • To evaluate the application, interview questions were asked periodically and participants were asked to vocalize as much as they could

Procedure

Procedure

  • One participant per session
  • Participant completes a lesson
  • Interview questions
  • After all versions, final interview questions
  • One hour per session
  • Recorded in its entirety + researcher notes
The procedure was as follows
  • A single participant would join a session
  • We would share the emulator with them and attempt to handoff control
  • Participants would complete one of the three presented prototypes
  • After completing a lesson, they would answer a series of interview questions
  • After completing all three versions, a round of final interview questions were administered
  • These sessions typically lasted about one hour
  • And, were recorded in their entirety. Researchers also took notes during these sessions

    Emotional Reinforcement

    • Virtual pet
    • Happiness increases with training
    • Happiness decays over time
    • Maintaining happiness
    • Intrinsic motivation
    • Users were provided a virtual pet
    • Their pet becomes happier as the user completes trainings
    • Happiness decays as a function of time, so if a user does not complete a training, their pet becomes sad
    • The question here was whether this virtual pet could act as a form of emotional reinforcement to engage users and motivate them to return to the application

    Grounding Activities

    • Programmatic breaks
    • Extremely difficult content
    • Emotionally triggering
    • First-hand experience
    • This prototype proposed building in programmatic breaks throughout the application
    • this would provide users with an opportunity to ground themselves
    • the content in the application can be extremely challenging for some people
    • and, may even risk triggering certain individuals
    • Especially those with first hand experience

    Interactive Elements

    • Constant interaction
    • Increased engagement
    • Flagging abuse / not abuse
    • Exploring the impact of constant interaction
    • hopes that this would increase engagement
    • Modeled after the a&a training exercise involving flagging behavior
    • Users watch a video and mark it as abuse / not abuse
    • After the video, if they succesfully identified the abusive segment, they were asked what type of abuse
    • If they failed to select the proper segment, they were offered redemption, then brought to slides if they answered incorrectly

    Quizzes

    • Presented a traditional quiz
    • Prompting users to think
    • Only quizzes
    • This prototype explored the effectiveness of presenting a quiz to users
    • the goal was to get users to reflect on their knowledge
    • Similar to most other quiz applications, presented a multiple-choice question
    • This prototype explored providing only quizzes

    Skills Activities

    • Tangential skills
    • Variety of activities
    • No Touch Zones: Sexual Abuse
    • Money Counting: Financial Abuse
    • Identifying Emotions: More General
    • This prototype asked a different question than the previous four
    • Would it be effective to just teach tangential skills?
    • It offered several different activities
    • No touch zones focused on sexual abuse by asking users to identify the no-touch zones on a human figure
    • Money counting asked users to make change up to a specified amount using US denominations
    • Identifying emotions asked users to identify emotions presented on screen
